Weight Management

5 Signs You're Under-eating (Not Overeating)

Plateaued progress and constant fatigue are often blamed on "cheating," but for many clients, the real problem is eating too little for too long.

Read more

When weight loss stalls, the instinct is almost always to cut further. But chronic under-eating slows metabolism, disrupts hormones, and makes binge-restrict cycles more likely, not less. Signs to watch for: constant hunger followed by low-energy crashes, poor sleep, irritability around food, losing your period or noticing hormonal shifts, and progress that stalls despite "doing everything right." The fix usually isn't eating less, it's eating enough, consistently, to let your body trust that food is reliable again. A personalized plan accounts for your actual energy needs instead of an arbitrary calorie number pulled from an app.

Medical Nutrition

PCOS and Carbs: Separating Fact from Fear

"Cut all carbs" is common PCOS advice, and it's usually wrong. Here's what actually helps with insulin resistance.

Read more

PCOS is closely linked to insulin resistance, which has led to a lot of blanket "no carbs" advice online. In practice, the type, timing, and pairing of carbohydrates matters far more than eliminating them. Pairing carbs with protein and fibre, spacing meals to avoid long fasting-then-overeating cycles, and prioritizing whole-food carbohydrate sources over ultra-processed ones all support better insulin sensitivity, without the unsustainable restriction that often backfires into bingeing. Every case of PCOS presents differently, so this is a starting framework, not a prescription. Work with a professional to individualize it.

Sports Nutrition

Post-Workout Nutrition: What Actually Matters

The "30-minute anabolic window" is mostly a myth. Here's what to actually prioritize after training.

Read more

Research has walked back the idea that you must eat within 30 minutes of finishing a workout or lose all your gains. What matters more: total daily protein intake, getting a balanced meal within a few hours of training, and rehydrating properly. For most recreational athletes, consistent daily nutrition, enough protein, enough carbohydrate to fuel training, and enough total calories, outweighs the exact timing of any single post-workout meal. Timing becomes more relevant for multi-session training days or competitive athletes, where a coach can help fine-tune it.

Family Nutrition

Postpartum Nutrition: Rebuilding Without the Rush

Postpartum bodies aren't a project to "bounce back" from. Nutrition here is about recovery first.

Read more

The postpartum period asks a lot of the body, healing, possibly breastfeeding, and surviving on interrupted sleep. Nutrition advice that pushes rapid weight loss during this window often ignores recovery needs entirely. Prioritizing enough total food (especially protein and iron), simple low-effort meals that survive being eaten one-handed, and hydration matters more here than calorie counting. Weight change, if desired, is a conversation for later, once recovery is stable and, if breastfeeding, milk supply isn't at risk. Every parent's timeline is different, and that's okay.

Nutrition Myths

5 Nutrition Myths That Refuse to Die

Carbs after 6pm, detox teas, "all fat is bad." Here's what the evidence actually says about the diet advice that won't quit.

Read more

Myth 1: eating carbs at night makes you gain weight, your body doesn't track the clock, total daily intake matters far more than timing. Myth 2: detox teas and juice cleanses "flush toxins," your liver and kidneys already do that job, these products mostly just cause water loss and bathroom trips. Myth 3: all fat is bad, healthy fats from nuts, olive oil, and fish support hormone health and satiety. Myth 4: skipping meals speeds weight loss, it usually backfires into overeating later and disrupts energy levels. Myth 5: you need to be sore to know a workout "worked," soreness has little to do with progress. Good nutrition advice is boring and consistent, not a trend.

Nutrition Myths

Diabetes and Diet Myths, Debunked

"Diabetics can't eat rice or fruit." Most of what circulates about diabetes and diet is outdated or just wrong.

Read more

Myth 1: people with diabetes can't eat rice, potatoes, or fruit, it's about portion, pairing, and timing, not blanket bans. A moderate portion of rice paired with protein, fibre, and vegetables affects blood sugar very differently than the same rice eaten alone. Myth 2: sugar-free products are automatically safe, many still spike blood sugar or contain carbohydrates from other sources. Myth 3: going very low-carb is the only way to manage blood sugar, sustainable, individualized plans usually beat extreme restriction long-term. Myth 4: fruit is off-limits, whole fruit with fibre is very different from fruit juice. Managing diabetes well is about consistent, personalized choices, not a list of banned foods.
